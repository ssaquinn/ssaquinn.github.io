Ethical Decisions:

Working with difficult people

I once was working in a team, where one of the team members would have mood swings and act disrespectfully towards others. She would become angry at team members for illogical reasons. Her behavior would reflect this by ignoring and refusing to engage with the team when the target member was around. I dealt with this by ignoring her behavior, trying to include her without pushing, still maintaining my own positivity and integrity, and trying to separate her and her target. I believe you must be very careful about confronting people who exhibit poor emotional awareness, as it can lead to them becoming defensive and worsening their behavior. If possible, I think it's best to influence people with positive example.

On the flip side of that, her attitude was never really dealt with, and she continued to bring negative energy to the team, which overtime had a negative effect on everyone's wellbeing. I think if I was in that situation again, I would still maintain my core strategy, however I would make our manager aware of her behavior to prevent her from escalating. I didn't make my manager aware at the time because I was attempting to be easy-going (haha I also didn't want to be a narc). I would also consider talking to the difficult co-worker; however, I would be very careful to do it from a place of kindness (asking her if she's ok, and if there's anything I can do to help her) and try not to come across as condescending or anything that would provoke her.

Emotional intelligence

Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligenceEmotional intelligence is the ability to understand, process and express emotions in a appropriate and constructive way. It also compasses the ability to recognise and have empathy towards others emotions and situations.

Emotional Intelligence vs Intellect

Intellect (not IQ -as these have been shown to be an inappropriate and biased test) can be seen as the brains ability to process information. Similar to emotional intelligence, however it deals with more physical/practical forms of data. Where as emotional intelligence is focused on the processing of emotional data.

The Importance of Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligence is important in regulating:

  • Self Management
  • Self Awareness
  • Social Awareness
  • Relationship Management

Through these attributes, you are able to take care of your own wellbeing and communicate issues effectivley. This allows you to be a reliable member of a team. You are also able to empathize and communicate clearly with others allowing for positive interactions, problem solving and conflict resolution.

NeuroPlasticity & The Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the ability of the brain to change structure to adapt in behaviour in response to a new experience. It is based on the idea that neural pathways within the brain will strengthen when a behaviour or thought pattern is repeated and weaken when the behaviour is avoided. In turn the stronger the neural pathway, the easier it is to perform the related task, creating a positive feedback loop. Neuroplasticity can occur just by understanding this, as the natural resistance or difficulty can be reframed as the initial learning phase, and it can be understood that the task will become easier overtime with persistence.

Neuroplasticity can help people achieve their goals, excel in their field of work or study, enhance wellbeing and quality or life and recover from both physical and mental trauma.

Neuroplasticity Techniques

  • Persistence and repetition.
  • Living a healthy lifestyle: good food, sleep, and physical activity
  • Always be learning; A new language, instrument or dance
  • Practice with your non-dominant side: for your sport, writing, or just general daily tasks - This has been shown to create new neural pathways and strengthen your ability for both sides
  • Learn to juggle
  • Use visualisation
  • Play Tetris and Super Mario - no, you are not a Manchild! You are being a responsible adult looking after your brain health!
  • Meditate
  • Practice breathing exercises

Growth Mindset

The term of the growth mindset was first coined by Dr Carol Dwek and it is thought of as the belief system about human's ability to learn and a dapt. It is in contrast to the idea of a fixed mindset which is the believe a person's learning and intelligence is fixed and inherent. The growth mindset is important because it affects our ability to achieve goals. Many studies on education have shown that if a student understands the concept that human intelligence is malleable and can be developed, then the student's motivation and achievement is likely to increase.
